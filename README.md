## What is an AI Studio Challenge Project?

AI Studio Challenge Projects are **real-world machine learning projects** presented by industry partner organizations. They are designed to give student participants ("fellows") an experience comparable to an undergraduate-level technical internship.

Once accepted, your project — along with a volunteer **Challenge Advisor** from your organization — will be assigned to a team of fellows to work on from **August to December 2026**.

---

## Project Essentials

Your proposed project should:

✅ Be **technical** in nature and involve **Machine Learning** to address a real business or social challenge

✅ Ideally produce artifacts your organization can actually use (e.g., an initial ML model for an engineering team)

✅ Be solvable primarily in **Python** — the one language all fellows share

✅ Build on the fellows'[ML Foundations](#ml-foundations) while introducing new, feasible challenges

✅ Cover **multiple stages of the ML lifecycle**: business understanding → data preparation → modeling → evaluation *(deployment not required)

✅ Be scoped for a part-time, unpaid, extracurricular team (~3 hours/week over ~3 months)

✅ Be **open source** — suitable for fellows to discuss publicly on GitHub and LinkedIn

---

## Problem Definition Requirements

Your submission must include:

1. **A clear project prompt** structured like:
   > *"In this project, you will use [type of data] and [ML techniques] to [outcome — e.g., build a model to predict X]. This will help our organization address [business problem]."*

2. **A dataset** that is publicly available or provided by your organization *(see [Dataset Requirements](#dataset-requirements) below)*

3. **Specific monthly milestones** that break the project into distinct phases
   > Example: *September: Data exploration and preprocessing*

---

## Dataset Requirements

Your dataset must be ready for the student team to use **as soon as AI Studio kicks off in mid-August**. Fellows should not be asked to find or scrape their own data.

### ✅ Must Meet These Criteria

| Requirement | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| **Accessible** | Publicly available (Kaggle, Hugging Face, Data.gov, etc.) or provided by your org |
| **No sensitive data** | No confidential, proprietary, or PII data. All such information must be removed, anonymized, or redacted |
| **No credentials needed** | Students cannot sign NDAs or require special permissions to access data |
| **Documented** | Must include a data dictionary with feature definitions, data types, preprocessing notes, and known limitations |
| **Sufficient size & quality** | Enough samples and complexity to support meaningful analysis; reasonable representation across categories |
| **Machine-readable** | CSV, JSON, or similar formats |
| **Size limit** | No larger than **10 GB** |

### 💡 Best Practices

- Prefer real-world data over synthetically generated data
- Data should require a **reasonable amount of preprocessing** by the student team — this is part of the learning experience
- Optimize for use in **low-resource environments** (e.g., Google Colab free tier)

---

## Resource Considerations

Projects should be feasible on the **free tier of Google Colab** (or an equivalent no-cost environment).

| Scenario | Requirement |
|----------|------------|
| Project needs GPU/TPU beyond Colab free tier | Your org must provide credits at no cost to students |
| Project requires deployment or significant compute | Your org must provide resources (e.g., AWS, platform hosting) at no cost |
| Project uses LLMs | Your org must provide API keys |

<details> 
  <summary><strong>What do Fellows learn in the Machine Learning Summer Course?</strong></summary>

  Fellows learn the end-to-end machine learning lifecycle, including data analysis, exploratory data analysis, feature engineering, model training and evaluation, and model selection. 

  The course also covers supervised learning, logistic regression, natural language processing (NLP), neural networks, and sentiment analysis. 

  In addition, Fellows are introduced to generative AI tools, prompt and context engineering, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems, and AI agents. They gain experience with Git-based collaboration, technical documentation, and approaches to identifying and mitigating bias in machine learning models.
